The tells

Six moves nearly every scam makes

The story changes. The mechanics rarely do. When two or more of these show up together, slow down.

"Act now or lose it"

Urgency is the engine of fraud. Deadlines, suspended accounts, and threats of arrest all exist to push you past the point where you would normally pause and check.

Strange ways to pay

Gift cards, crypto, wire transfers, or a payment app to a stranger. No real business asks for these.

Too good to be true

Guaranteed returns, a prize you never entered, a job that pays a lot for almost nothing.

"Keep this between us"

Secrecy isolates you from the people, like your bank, who would spot the trick.

Contact you never started

A surprise text, DM, or call about a problem you did not know you had.

"Let's move off here"

Pushing the chat to WhatsApp or Telegram escapes a platform's fraud protections.

The scams you are most likely to meet

Different masks, same handful of tells underneath.

Phishing & smishing

Fake emails and texts posing as your bank, a delivery service, or a tax office to harvest logins.

Romance scams

Weeks of warmth, then a sudden emergency that only your money can fix. The person rarely video-calls.

Investment & crypto

A new "friend" or "advisor" shows fake gains on a slick app, then locks your withdrawal behind more fees.

Tech-support scams

A pop-up or call claims your device is infected, then talks you into remote access or a "refund".

Marketplace & overpayment

A buyer "accidentally" overpays and asks for the difference back, or a listing vanishes once you pay.

How a scam actually unfolds

Seeing the shape of it makes the next one easier to catch.

  1. 01

    Contact

    A message arrives that you did not ask for.

  2. 02

    Build trust

    They sound official, friendly, or romantic.

  3. 03

    Apply pressure

    A deadline or threat appears out of nowhere.

  4. 04

    Extract

    Money or login details change hands.

  5. 05

    Vanish

    Contact stops, or new fees appear forever.

If it happens to you

You are not the first, and it is not too late

Scams are built by professionals to fool careful people. Skip the shame, move fast, and work the list.

In the first hour

  • Stop all contact. Do not send another payment to "release" the first one. That is the trap closing.

  • Call your bank or card issuer. Ask to stop or reverse the payment and freeze the account if needed.

  • Change passwords, turn on 2FA. Start with email and banking. Use a different password for each.

  • Screenshot everything. Messages, numbers, profiles, and receipts. You will need them to report.

Then report it

  • U.S. FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov

  • FBI IC3 for online crime at ic3.gov

  • The platform. Report the profile or listing where you met them so others are warned.

  • A credit bureau. Consider a fraud alert or freeze if any personal details were shared.

Outside the U.S.? Search "report a scam" with your country to find the local authority.
